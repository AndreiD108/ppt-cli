# Creative Direction

A creative deck is one where the presentation itself is part of the message. The slides are not a vehicle for information delivery — they are an experience. The audience should feel something before they understand something. Impact first, detail second.

This direction suits design showcases, portfolio reviews, brand launches, event keynotes, product reveals, campaign pitches, and any context where forgettable slides are worse than no slides at all. The presenter is not reporting — they are persuading, inspiring, or provoking.

---

## Palette character

Creative palettes are confident. They commit to a mood rather than hedging toward neutrality. Think of the color palette of a gallery exhibition poster — deliberate, atmospheric, immediately communicating a sensibility. The colors should feel like an artistic choice, not a safe default.

The palette's emotional register should match the subject. A brand launch for a luxury product wants deep, rich tones — burgundy, black, gold. A festival keynote wants vibrancy — saturated coral, electric blue, warm yellow. A design portfolio wants restraint that lets the work speak — near-black with a single vivid accent.

Saturation is a tool, not a constant. A fully saturated palette is energetic but exhausting over many slides. The stronger move is often a muted or dark primary with one highly saturated accent — the contrast makes the accent hit harder.

Example palettes that illustrate the range:

## Typography

Creative decks demand typographic personality. The header font is not just legible — it is expressive. It sets a tone the moment the first slide appears. The body font recedes, but it should complement rather than clash.

Size contrast in creative decks runs higher than in other directions. Titles can push to 48-60pt or beyond. Body text can drop to 13-14pt. The gap between levels is what creates the visual tension — a large title and small body text feel composed, like a magazine spread. A large title and medium body text feel uncertain, like the hierarchy wasn't fully committed to.

Font pairings that suit creative decks:

- **Impact + Arial.** Maximum typographic aggression. The title dominates physically. Works for event decks, launches, anything that needs to feel loud.
- **Palatino + Garamond.** Elegant and editorial. Both serif, but Palatino is sturdier for headers while Garamond is refined for body. Works for portfolios, luxury, culture.
- **Georgia + Calibri.** Warm serif header with a clean modern body. The contrast between serif and sans creates gentle visual interest. Works for storytelling, brand narrative.
- **Trebuchet MS + Calibri.** Friendly and approachable but still distinctive. Works for product stories, team-oriented narratives, community events.

Italic is a real tool in this direction. Use it for pull quotes, taglines, and single-line statements that anchor a slide. A large italic sentence centered on a colored background is one of the strongest creative slide patterns.

## Background strategy

Creative decks favor boldness over safety. Two approaches work:

**Committed dark.** Dark backgrounds throughout — deep navy, near-black, charcoal. Text is light (white or near-white for titles, a muted light tone for body). Images pop against the dark field. This approach feels premium and focused. It works especially well when the deck is image-heavy, because photographs and illustrations gain visual weight against dark surrounds.

**Alternating contrast.** Dark slides for structural moments (title, section dividers, conclusion) and light or colored slides for content. The alternation creates rhythm — the audience feels the deck breathe between sections. Use this when the content needs room (data, text-heavy explanations) but the transitions should still feel dramatic.

What does not work in this direction: all-white or all-light backgrounds. A creative deck on a white background looks like a document, not an experience. If the content needs a light background for readability, use an off-white or tinted tone rather than pure white, and ensure the surrounding structural slides provide enough visual contrast.

## Layout preferences

The creative direction reaches heavily for image-dominant layouts. The primary patterns:

**Full-bleed background image.** The signature move. A photograph or illustration fills the entire slide. Text is overlaid — either positioned in negative space within the image, or placed on a semi-transparent color bar that sits over part of the image. This is the default for title slides and section dividers. Choose images with clear negative space areas, or specify negative space in AI generation prompts.

**Half-bleed image.** An image fills one half of the slide (left, right, top, or bottom) and content occupies the other half. This is the workhorse content layout — it keeps every slide visually rich without requiring full-bleed treatment. Vary which half the image occupies from slide to slide.

**Quote or callout.** A single statement, large and centered (or asymmetrically placed), often in italic, on a colored or dark background. No supporting content, no images — just the words. This works for section transitions, key takeaways, and provocative questions. Its power comes from contrast with the image-heavy slides around it.

**Stat callout.** An oversized number in the deck's most expressive font, paired with a short label. In the creative direction, stat callouts are visual events — the number might be 80pt+, in the accent color, positioned asymmetrically. They are not data tables; they are punctuation.

Layouts this direction uses sparingly:

- **Icon + text rows** — too structured and uniform for creative decks unless the icons are distinctive and hand-crafted in style.
- **Grids** — can work if each cell is visually rich (image + text), but a grid of text-only cells feels corporate.
- **Tables** — almost never appropriate. If there is tabular data, find a visual way to present it (stat callouts, comparison images, infographic-style layouts).

The universal rule about layout variation is especially important here. A creative deck that uses the same full-bleed image layout on every slide is not creative — it is a photo slideshow. Alternate between full-bleed, half-bleed, quote slides, and stat callouts to create visual rhythm.

## Visual motif

Creative decks benefit most from a motif that is itself visually interesting — not just functional.

Motif principles that suit this direction:

- **Bold blocks of color.** Large filled shapes as section backgrounds, oversized colored rectangles partially behind images, thick accent bars that feel architectural rather than decorative. The color blocks become compositional elements, not just containers.
- **Cinematic framing.** Images treated as if they are film stills — wide aspect ratios, letterbox-style black bars above and below, or images that deliberately bleed off one edge. The motif principle is "this is a visual story."
- **Typographic weight.** The motif is the typography itself — oversized words used as visual elements, text that fills the slide, letters cropped at the edge. Works when the content is punchy and the message is built around short, powerful statements.

For high-coverage anchoring, consider a consistent background treatment (a subtle gradient direction, a tinted overlay on all images, a dark border on all slides) or a small brand element in a fixed position.

## Content density

Less. Creative decks communicate through impression, not information density. Each slide should make one point, supported by one strong visual.

Guidelines:

- **Title slides**: one line, maybe two. No subtitle paragraphs.
- **Content slides**: a headline plus 2-4 short lines of text maximum. If you have more to say, use another slide.
- **Data slides**: one stat, one insight. Not a dashboard — a spotlight.
- **Quote slides**: one sentence. Attribution if needed. Nothing else.

If the user's content is dense (a lot of information to convey), the creative approach is to spread it across more slides rather than cramming it into fewer. Twenty slides with one idea each are more watchable than ten slides with two ideas each.

Speaker notes carry the detail. The slides carry the impact. Anything the presenter needs to say that doesn't fit on the slide goes in the notes.

## Image style

Images are not decorations in creative decks — they are primary content. Every image should feel like it was chosen (or generated) for this specific slide, not pulled from a generic library.

**Photography** is the default for creative decks that deal with real subjects (people, places, products, events). Lean toward editorial and cinematic styles: shallow depth of field, intentional lighting, color grading that matches the palette. Avoid flat corporate photography — it undermines the creative direction immediately.

Style modifiers that suit this direction:
- `editorial photography, cinematic color grading` — magazine-quality, moody
- `fashion magazine style editorial` — bold, stylized
- `shot on medium-format analog film, pronounced grain` — textured, editorial
- `Chiaroscuro lighting with harsh, high contrast` — dramatic
- `golden hour backlighting creating long shadows` — warm, cinematic

Beyond specific modifiers, brand aesthetics and named artistic references (see `2-image-generation.md`) are especially powerful style anchors in creative decks — this direction has the widest stylistic latitude, so the full range of style vocabulary is available to you.

**Illustration** works for conceptual content, section dividers, and decks where photography would feel too literal. Use styles with visual character — watercolor, high-fidelity 3D renders, bold flat illustration with limited palettes. Avoid generic clip-art aesthetics.

**Genre and stylized rendering** is where this direction has latitude that others do not. Manga, comic book, retro poster, pixel art, propaganda art, vintage travel poster — these are communicative tools, not novelties. Manga exaggerates motion and emotion in ways photography cannot; comic panels depict cause-and-effect sequences in a single image; retro and stylized art creates engagement through surprise, because the genre is unexpected in a slide context. Reach for these when the tone is informal, the audience will appreciate personality, or the content benefits from emotional amplification — a team retrospective with dramatic moments, a product launch with a "battle" framing, an offsite invitation as a vintage travel poster. Some of these genres (retro posters, comic panels, magazine covers, propaganda art) will produce text in the image whether or not you ask for it. Specify the exact text you want in quotes, or explicitly suppress it with `"no visible text"` if you want the style without the typography. See `2-image-generation.md` for the full genre inventory and prompt guidance.

**AI-generated text in images** is a powerful tool in creative decks. A title slide with the presentation name rendered directly into a full-bleed image (large bold sans-serif on a dramatic background) can be more impactful than a text shape overlaid on an image. Use `--reasoning` for these — text rendering benefits from the model's extra planning.

Always specify composition in prompts. Creative slides frequently need negative space for text overlay — include `negative space on the left for text` or `subject positioned in the lower third` in the prompt. If an image will be half-bleed, specify the aspect ratio accordingly.

Resolution: use `2k` for any final full-bleed or half-bleed image. Use `1k` for smaller inline images. `512` is for drafting iterations only — never for final placed images. The pixel density gap is most visible in creative decks, where images are large and central.

## Anti-patterns

Things that break the creative direction:

| Pattern | Why it fails | What to do instead |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Bullet-point lists as the primary content structure | Feels like a business deck wearing a creative skin | Use separate text shapes, visual layouts, or image-text pairings |
| White backgrounds on content slides | Drains the visual energy; looks like a document | Use dark, tinted, or colored backgrounds |
| Generic stock-photo aesthetic (smiling professionals in a bright office) | Undermines creative credibility instantly | Use editorial photography, cinematic lighting, or illustration |
| Every slide has the same layout | A photo slideshow, not a presentation | Alternate between full-bleed, half-bleed, quote, stat, and text layouts |
| Small, cautious images (thumbnails in a corner) | Wastes the primary creative tool | Make images large — half-bleed minimum, full-bleed when possible |
| Text-heavy slides with no visual hierarchy | Audience reads instead of watches; loses the room | One point per slide, strong size contrast, let images carry weight |
| Accent line under the title | Generic template signal; especially damaging in a creative context | Use whitespace, background contrast, or typographic scale to separate title from content |
| Safe, muted palette with no accent | Looks timid, not tasteful | Commit to at least one strong accent color; restraint means selective boldness, not absence of boldness |

## Non-obvious command patterns

A few ppt-cli workflows that are particularly relevant to the creative direction and may not be immediately apparent from `--help`.

**Full-slide background from an AI-generated image.** Generate an image sized to fill the slide, place it at origin, then layer text on top with contrasting font colors. The image must be added before the text shapes so it sits behind them in the layer order.

**Semi-transparent color overlay on images.** There is no opacity control in ppt-cli. To achieve the effect of text readable over a busy image, generate the image with a natural dark area or gradient (specify this in the prompt), then position text in that area. Alternatively, add a narrow colored bar (a textbox with a fill and no text) and place text on top of it.

**Oversized stat numbers as visual elements.** Add a textbox with font size 80-96pt and bold. The number itself becomes the visual element on the slide — no image needed. Pair with a small label (12-14pt, muted color) positioned directly below.

**Asymmetric text placement.** Not all text needs to start at x=0.5in. In creative layouts, placing a title at x=2in or x=3in with a large left margin creates visual tension and negative space. The universal 0.5" margin is the minimum, not the default starting position.
