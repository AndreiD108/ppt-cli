# Educational Direction

A presentation in the educational direction is a teaching instrument. Its job is not to impress or persuade — it is to transfer understanding. Every design choice serves clarity: can the audience follow the sequence, locate themselves within the structure, and distinguish what they need to remember from what is supporting context?

The defining quality is predictability. Where other directions use layout variation to maintain visual interest, educational decks use layout consistency to reduce cognitive overhead. The audience is processing new information on every slide — the last thing they need is to also figure out where the title moved or why this slide looks different from the last one. When the structure is predictable, the audience spends their attention on the content.

---

## Palette character

Educational palettes are warm, approachable, and low-tension. Think of the color scheme of a well-designed textbook page — inviting, organized, easy on the eyes for extended reading. They avoid the gravity of dark corporate tones and the intensity of bold creative palettes. The primary color should feel inviting rather than authoritative — teal, warm blue, soft green, muted orange. The secondary color provides structure (card backgrounds, section fills) without competing. The accent marks the things the audience must retain — key terms, important numbers, callout boxes.

Saturation should be moderate. Fully saturated primaries feel aggressive in a learning context; very desaturated ones feel lifeless. The sweet spot is colors that feel friendly and energetic without being loud.

## Typography

The header font should feel clear and confident without being imposing. Strong sans-serifs work well — they read easily at a distance and don't carry the formality of serifs or the austerity of geometric fonts. The body font must be highly readable at 14-16pt, since educational slides carry more text than most directions.

Pairings that fit the educational character:

- **Trebuchet MS / Calibri** — Friendly and approachable. Trebuchet has enough personality to mark headers without feeling decorative. Good default when unsure.
- **Trebuchet MS / Verdana** — Warmer and rounder than the Calibri pairing. Verdana's generous letter spacing reads well at smaller sizes and on screens. Good for corporate training and onboarding where readability over long sessions matters.
- **Calibri / Calibri Light** — Clean and neutral. Works when the content is already complex enough without the typography adding character. The weight contrast between Calibri and Calibri Light is subtle but sufficient.
- **Georgia / Calibri** — Adds warmth through the serif header. Works for humanities, social science, and any topic where the content benefits from a slightly literary feel.
- **Arial Black / Arial** — Direct and no-nonsense. Works for technical training where the focus is on procedure, not personality.

Avoid: Impact (too aggressive), Palatino/Garamond (too editorial — the audience is learning, not reading a magazine), Consolas (too technical unless the subject is literally programming).

### Size tuning

Educational decks can push body text to 15-16pt rather than 14pt, since there is more of it per slide and it needs to stay comfortable. Titles should be at the lower end of the universal range (36-38pt) — large enough to anchor the slide but not so large they consume space needed for content.

Section headers within a slide (20-24pt) are particularly important in this direction. They break content into scannable chunks, and the audience uses them as navigation aids. Do not skip them.

## Background strategy

Light backgrounds throughout. Educational decks should feel open and readable. Dark backgrounds are acceptable only for the title slide and, optionally, section dividers — but the content slides should be light.

The content slide background is typically white or a very faint tint of the primary color (a 5-10% opacity wash that adds warmth without reducing contrast). Avoid pure white if the palette is warm — a barely-there cream or warm gray prevents the coldness of a blank white slide.

Section dividers, when used, benefit from a primary-color fill with white text. This creates a clear visual break that tells the audience "we are moving to a new topic" without needing to read the text. The color shift does the signaling.

## Layout preferences

Educational decks draw heavily from a subset of the universal layout vocabulary. The key patterns:

**Icon + text rows** — the workhorse layout for this direction. Three or four items per slide, each with a small icon or colored marker, a bold label, and a one-to-two line description. This pattern maps directly to how people learn: discrete, labeled chunks that can be processed one at a time.

**Timeline or process flow** — for any sequential content (steps, phases, procedures, chronological narratives). Numbered steps with consistent visual structure. Each step should be visually distinct (its own shape group) so the audience can see how many steps there are and where they are in the sequence.

**Two-column** — useful for concept + example, theory + practice, or text + illustration pairings. In educational context, the image/illustration side should be genuinely explanatory, not decorative. If the image doesn't help the audience understand the text, it doesn't belong.

**Grid (2x2 or 2x3)** — for categorizations, comparisons, and overview slides. Each cell should have identical internal structure (icon, title, short description) so the audience reads them as a set rather than four unrelated items.

**Callout or key takeaway** — a single important statement, visually distinguished from regular content. In educational decks, these are "remember this" moments — definitions, rules, formulas, key principles. They deserve their own slide or a prominent callout box within a content slide.

Patterns used less often in this direction: full-bleed background images (too atmospheric, not informational enough), asymmetric layouts (unpredictable, increases cognitive load), stat callouts (rarely the right fit unless the subject is data-oriented).

### Layout consistency over variation

The universal rules say never repeat the same layout on consecutive slides. Educational decks relax this rule. If three consecutive slides each present a concept using icon + text rows, that repetition is a feature — the audience learns the pattern on the first slide and processes the second and third faster because the structure is familiar.

Vary layouts between *sections* of the deck, not necessarily between every slide. A section on "key principles" might use four icon + text slides in a row. The next section on "implementation steps" switches to a timeline layout. The variety comes at the section level, not the slide level.

## Visual motif

Educational decks benefit from high-coverage motifs that provide structural cues:

**Progress indicators.** A section number or progress bar in a consistent position on every slide. This tells the audience where they are in the overall structure — "Section 2 of 5" or a simple filled/unfilled dot sequence. This is one of the most powerful educational motifs because it directly serves the audience's need to feel oriented.

**Consistent callout boxes.** A colored container (filled rectangle with the accent or a tinted version of the primary) used to highlight key takeaways, definitions, or important notes. When the audience sees this shape, they know "this is something I should remember." The shape becomes a signal.

**Numbered labels.** When content is sequential, a number inside a small filled circle (primary color, white text) at the start of each item. The visual principle is "ordered, labeled structure." This manifests as step numbers in process slides, section numbers on dividers, and item numbers in lists.

**Icon style consistency.** If icons are used (and they should be, frequently), they should all share a visual style — all line-art, all filled, all inside circles, all the same size. Mixing icon styles (one realistic, one flat, one outlined) breaks the sense of a coherent system.

The visual principle for educational motifs is generally "structured and signposted." Every recurring element should help the audience navigate — where am I, what kind of content is this, what should I pay attention to.

## Content density

Educational decks tolerate more text per slide than other directions, but "more" is relative. A slide with 80 words of well-structured content (bold labels, short descriptions, clear hierarchy) is fine. A slide with 80 words in a single unparsed paragraph is not.

The key is structure, not brevity. An educational slide can have five items where a business slide would have three — as long as each item is clearly delineated (its own shape, its own label, consistent spacing).

### How many slides per topic

Prefer more slides with less content each over fewer slides packed with content. Splitting a complex topic across three slides (setup, explanation, takeaway) is easier to follow than cramming it into one. The audience processes one idea at a time, and each slide transition is a natural pause.

A rough guideline: if a slide has more than four distinct content blocks (not counting the title), consider splitting it. If you find yourself shrinking font sizes to fit content, you definitely need to split.

### Speaker notes

Educational presentations benefit heavily from speaker notes. The slides should contain the structure and key points; the speaker notes should contain the full explanation, examples, transitions, and context that the presenter delivers verbally. Do not put the presenter's script on the slides — that defeats the purpose of the visual medium.

## Image style

Educational presentations use images as explanatory aids, not atmospheric elements. Every image should help the audience understand something — a concept, a process, a real-world example of an abstract idea.

**Illustrations and diagrams** over photography. Flat vector illustrations, simple diagrams, and schematic visuals communicate more clearly than photographs in most educational contexts. A diagram of a process is more useful than a stock photo of people working.

**Icons as visual shorthand.** Small, consistent icons next to list items or section headers act as visual labels. They help the audience scan and locate content. Use a consistent style (all line-art, or all flat filled) and a consistent size (0.4-0.6" square).

**Screenshots and real examples** when teaching tools or procedures. Annotated screenshots — with callout arrows, numbered labels, or highlighted regions — are more useful than describing the interface in text.

**Photography** when showing real-world context — a photo of the work environment being discussed, the equipment being trained on, the people involved. Avoid generic stock photography that illustrates a vague concept ("teamwork," "innovation"). If the photo could illustrate any topic, it is not specific enough for an educational deck.

When generating images with AI, the guiding principle is clarity over atmosphere — but "clarity" does not mean "flat vector and nothing else." Prefer styles that prioritize readability and explanation: flat vector illustration, clean isometric rendering (useful for spatial and architectural concepts), simplified diagrammatic compositions, or pictographic styles (Isotype-style for data, geometric reduction for concepts). The right style depends on the subject — an isometric 3D cutaway of a machine serves an engineering training better than a flat vector would; a pictographic statistical comparison serves a public health workshop better than a bar chart. Prompts should specify informational intent: `"clean isometric diagram of a three-layer network architecture, labeled components, minimal palette"` rather than `"beautiful abstract visualization of networking."`

When a diagram is complex enough that building it from shapes would be fragile or hard to label clearly — multi-step processes with arrows, feedback loops, system architectures with many components — generate it as an image instead. The model handles spatial organization, labeled components, and visual hierarchy natively, and the result at 1k or 2k resolution is often cleaner than manual shape construction. This is especially relevant for educational decks, which have a higher density of explanatory diagrams than any other direction.

When teaching tools or procedures, annotated screenshots are one of the strongest educational image types. You can pass a raw screenshot as `--ref` and prompt for annotations — callout arrows pointing to interface elements, numbered labels on key areas, highlighted regions — producing a polished annotated visual without manual graphic design.

When the content involves data — statistics, survey results, trends, comparisons — consider generating chart images rather than building tables or shape-based charts. Educational charts should teach, not just display: highlight the element the audience should focus on, use color contrast or annotation to draw attention to the lesson within the data, and provide enough context that the chart explains itself. A bar chart where the outlier bar is in the accent color with a callout label saying what it means is an educational chart. The same bars in uniform color with no emphasis is a data dump.

## Anti-patterns

Things that work in other directions but fail in educational decks:

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails here | What to do instead |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| All-dark backgrounds | Feels heavy and reduces readability for extended viewing; learners need open, well-lit visual space | Light backgrounds throughout; dark only for title slide and section dividers |
| Layout variety on every slide | Forces the audience to re-orient on each slide, competing with content for attention | Vary layouts between sections, not between every slide; consistency aids learning |
| Full-bleed images used for atmosphere rather than information | If an image fills the slide, it should be because the content requires the space — a complex diagram, a detailed illustrated scene that sets context. Atmospheric full-bleeds (mood photography, abstract gradients) waste space the audience needs for learning | Use full-bleed only when the visual is itself the information; otherwise use explanatory illustrations, diagrams, or annotated screenshots |
| Minimal text with heavy visual reliance | Educational slides are often reference artifacts; the audience needs enough text to reconstruct what was taught | Include structured text with bold labels and clear hierarchy; let speaker notes carry the full narrative |
| Stat callouts as primary layout | A giant number without context doesn't teach; the audience needs to know what it means and why it matters | Present numbers with surrounding explanation, context, and relationship to the topic |
| Decorative elements that don't signal structure | Every visual element should communicate information or signal structure; if it does neither, it competes with content for attention | Use visual elements that serve navigation — progress indicators, callout boxes, step markers |

## Non-obvious commands

A short reference for ppt-cli operations that are useful in educational decks but not immediately apparent from `--help`.

**Creating a colored callout box.** Add an empty textbox at the desired position, fill it with the accent color (or a light tint), then add the actual text as a separate shape layered on top. This gives you a colored background container — useful for key takeaways and definitions.

**Building a progress indicator.** Add a small textbox in a consistent corner position (e.g., bottom-right) on every slide, containing the section label or "Step N of M." Style it with a small font size (10-11pt) and the secondary or muted color so it is visible but not competing with content. Copy the same position coordinates to every slide for consistency.

**Numbered step markers.** For step numbers inside colored circles: add an empty textbox sized as a small square (0.4" x 0.4"), fill it with the primary color, then add the number as a separate textbox layered on top with white bold text and center alignment. Repeat with consistent positioning for each step.

**Icon placeholders.** When generating icons with AI image generation, use `--ratio 1:1`, `--resolution 512`, and a prompt specifying "flat vector icon, single color, white background, clean edges." Size them at 0.4-0.6" in the slide and position them with consistent left margins across all icon + text rows.
