# Business Direction

The business deck is the workhorse of corporate communication. It exists to deliver information clearly, build confidence in the narrative, and signal organizational competence. The audience — executives, board members, investors, department heads — is there to make decisions based on what the slides contain. They will notice the design, but what they should notice is precision: every element aligned, every choice deliberate, every slide consistent. That care is a trust signal — it implies the same rigor went into the numbers as into the formatting.

The defining quality is restraint. Every choice should feel deliberate and conservative — not boring, but controlled. The audience should feel they are looking at output from an organization that pays attention to detail, where the quality visible in the slides implies quality in the underlying work. A messy slide in a board meeting doesn't just look bad — it undermines confidence in everything on it.

## Palette character

Business palettes are muted, authoritative, and low-risk. Think of the color scheme in a premium financial dashboard or the cover of a well-designed annual report — restrained, deliberate, designed to be looked at for extended periods without fatigue. They lean toward cool tones — navy, charcoal, slate, deep teal — though warm neutrals (taupe, warm gray) work when the subject calls for it. Saturation is moderate to low. Nothing screams.

The accent color is where the palette earns its specificity. A navy-and-white deck is generic. A navy-and-white deck with a teal accent for key metrics feels chosen. The accent should be noticeable without being loud — think of it as the one color the audience remembers.

These are examples of the *kind* of palette to build, not a fixed menu. The right palette depends on the specific company, topic, and audience. A quarterly financial review might call for Midnight Executive; a sustainability report might call for something in the teal-green range; an internal strategy update might use Charcoal Minimal because the content is complex enough without color competing for attention.

In business contexts, the user's company often has existing brand colors. Ask about this before building a palette — brand guidelines frequently override direction-level suggestions, and the agent's job becomes fitting the brand colors into the dominance hierarchy rather than choosing new ones.

What unites them: muted primaries, high-contrast secondary for content areas, and a single accent that is used sparingly.

## Typography

Business typography is clean, readable, and hierarchically strict. The header font should signal competence — it can have personality, but not playfulness. The body font is purely functional.

Font pairings that work in this direction:

| Header | Body | When to use it |
|--------|------|----------------|
| Georgia | Calibri | The safe default for formal business. Georgia's serifs give titles warmth and authority. Calibri body is clean and widely rendered. Good for financial reports, board decks, investor updates. |
| Cambria | Calibri | More traditional than Georgia. Feels institutional — banks, law firms, established enterprises. The serif-to-sans transition from header to body creates clear hierarchy. |
| Arial Black | Arial | The no-nonsense corporate pair. No serifs, no flourish. Headers are blunt and heavy; body is invisible. Works when the content is purely data-driven and the typography should be transparent. |
| Calibri | Calibri Light | Modern minimalism. Same family, different weights. Feels clean and contemporary. Good for tech companies, startups that want to look corporate, modern internal comms. |

Avoid: Impact (reads as aggressive — it belongs on posters, not board decks), Trebuchet MS (reads as casual and approachable — fine for educational, wrong here), and any display or decorative fonts (they draw attention to the typography itself, which is the opposite of what business needs). The principle: if the audience notices the typeface, it is the wrong typeface for this direction.

### Size recommendations

Stick to the tighter end of the universal ranges. Business decks benefit from precision:

- Slide titles: 36-40pt. Rarely above 40 — oversized titles feel like marketing, not reporting.
- Body text: 14-15pt. Readable at projection distance, compact enough for data-dense slides.
- Stat callouts: 60-66pt. Large enough to function as visual elements, but not so large they feel decorative.
- Captions and sources: 10-11pt. Present but unobtrusive. Use a muted color (medium gray) to further reduce their visual weight.

## Background strategy

The contrast sandwich is the natural fit for business decks: dark backgrounds for the title slide, section dividers, and conclusion slide; light backgrounds (white or very light neutral) for all content slides. This creates a visual rhythm the audience can anticipate — dark slides signal structure (beginning, transitions, end), light slides signal content.

On dark slides, use white or very light text. On light slides, use dark text (near-black or the primary color at high value). The contrast must be unambiguous — business decks cannot afford readability issues.

An all-light approach also works, especially for shorter decks or internal documents where the dark bookends feel excessive. An all-dark approach is less common in business contexts and can feel more like a keynote or pitch — use it only if the audience and setting warrant it.

## Layout preferences

Business decks draw heavily from the structured end of the layout vocabulary. The audience expects information presented in a way they can parse quickly — grids, columns, labeled sections.

**Primary patterns:**

- **Stat callout.** The signature business layout. Two or three large numbers with short labels, arranged horizontally. This is how financial results, KPIs, and growth metrics should be presented — not buried in a table or a paragraph.
- **Comparison columns.** Side-by-side columns for before/after, plan vs. actual, Q1 vs. Q2. Consistent internal structure across columns so the audience can scan horizontally.
- **Two-column (text + data).** Narrative on the left, supporting data or chart on the right. The default for slides that need both explanation and evidence.
- **Grid (2x2).** Four content blocks with consistent structure. Works for team updates, department summaries, strategic pillars.

**Supporting patterns:**

- **Section divider.** Dark background, section title, optional section number. Keeps the audience oriented in longer decks (10+ slides).
- **Timeline.** Horizontal or vertical numbered steps. For roadmaps, project milestones, implementation plans.
- **Quote or callout.** Sparingly — for a key executive quote, a customer testimonial, or a strategic statement that deserves emphasis.

**Rarely used:**

- **Full-bleed background image.** Uncommon in business decks. If used, limited to the title slide. Content slides should not have photographic backgrounds — they compete with data.
- **Half-bleed image.** Occasionally for case studies or team slides. Not a primary pattern.
- **Asymmetric layouts.** Avoid. Business audiences expect predictable structure. Asymmetry reads as creative or informal.

## Visual motif

Business motifs should be subtle and high-coverage. The audience should perceive consistency without being able to point to a specific decorative element.

Motif principles that work:

- **Sharp and geometric.** Rectangular content cards with hard corners, thin accent bars along one edge of cards or sections, square image crops. Clean, precise, corporate. This is the most natural fit for data-heavy business decks.
- **Bold blocks of color.** Filled rectangles as section backgrounds, solid color bars for headers, content grouped inside colored containers. Creates strong visual structure without needing imagery.

High-coverage anchor elements for business decks:

- A small company logo (or a generated brand mark) in the bottom-right or bottom-left corner of every slide. This is standard in corporate settings and provides instant brand continuity.
- A thin accent-colored bar along the top or bottom edge of every content slide. Simple, unobtrusive, and consistent.
- A consistent header treatment — a dark-filled rectangle behind the slide title on every content slide, or the title always positioned in the same spot with the same background element.

The natural fit is geometric and precise — sharp corners, straight lines, rectangular containers. Rounded shapes are not off-limits but should be used deliberately, not as a default. Decorative illustrations and heavy border treatments are out of place. The motif should feel systematic, not designed.

## Content density

Business decks carry more information per slide than creative or educational decks, but the density must be organized, not dumped.

Guidelines:

- **One main point per slide.** Even on data-dense slides, there should be a clear takeaway. If a slide has three metrics, the title or a visual cue should indicate which one matters most.
- **Favor numbers over words.** Where possible, replace descriptive text with data. "Revenue grew significantly" is weaker than "$18.2M (+24%)." Business audiences trust numbers.
- **Executive summary slides.** For decks longer than 8-10 slides, include a summary slide near the front with the 3-5 key takeaways. This respects the audience's time and gives them the conclusion before the evidence.
- **Speaker notes carry the narrative.** The slides present the data; the speaker provides the interpretation. Use notes generously — they are where the nuanced explanation lives, not the slide surface.
- **Tables are acceptable here.** More so than in other directions. When the audience genuinely needs to cross-reference rows and columns (budget breakdowns, project status grids, quarterly comparisons), a table is the right choice. But for 2-4 data points, stat callouts are still better.

## Image style

Business decks use images sparingly and purposefully. Photography, when present, should be clean, professional, and supporting — never the focal point of a content slide.

When to use images:

- Title slide backgrounds (subtle, dark, blurred or abstract — not a busy photograph)
- Team or people slides (headshots, group photos)
- Case study slides (product photos, client logos)
- Charts and data visualizations

When generating images for a business deck, establish a shared style fragment tied to the palette and reuse it across all image prompts. The fragment should read as a narrative description covering lighting, color grading, and compositional approach — not a list of keywords. Examples matched to the palettes above:

- **Midnight Executive:** `"desaturated editorial photography, three-point softbox lighting, shallow depth of field, cool blue-gray color grading with dominant tones coordinated to navy #1E2761 and steel blue #7896BB, clean composition with generous negative space"`
- **Warm Slate:** `"professional photography, soft diffused natural window lighting, warm neutral color grading coordinated with taupe and warm gray #C4BAB0, muted earth tones, understated and quiet"`
- **Teal Trust:** `"clean corporate photography, even overcast-style lighting, muted teal color grading anchored to #004D5C, cool and restrained, minimal background detail"`

Adapt these to the specific palette in use — the point is the register (controlled, desaturated, editorially quiet), not these exact words. Beyond the style fragment:

- Keep subjects generic enough to feel universal — avoid overly specific scenarios that might not match the company's reality
- Abstract or geometric backgrounds work well for title and section slides — they add visual interest without making claims about content
- Use muted, desaturated tones that complement the palette rather than competing with it

Do not use text-in-image techniques for business decks. Embedded typography, text rendered as part of a visual composition, text-as-visual-element — these belong in creative directions where the artistic integration justifies the loss of control. Business decks need editable text in text shapes, where font, size, color, and position are precisely controlled and guaranteed readable. If a title slide uses a generated background image, the title is a text shape overlaid on the image, not rendered into it.

When not to use images:

- Data slides — with a distinction. Decorative imagery alongside actual data is redundant — the numbers are the visual, and a stock photograph of "a graph" next to a real chart adds nothing. However, for complex visualizations (multi-variable comparisons, dense trend analyses, detailed breakdowns), an AI-generated chart image can *replace* a shape-based chart when it would produce a cleaner, more polished result. The generated chart is the data visualization, not decoration alongside it.
- Process slides. Shapes and text structure communicate sequence better than illustrations in a business context.
- Any slide where the image would be purely decorative with no informational value.

## Anti-patterns

These are choices that are acceptable in other directions but wrong for business:

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails here | What to do instead |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Expressive or decorative typography | Signals informality; undermines credibility | Stick to conservative pairings with clear hierarchy |
| Full-bleed photography on content slides | Competes with data; makes the deck feel like a keynote or pitch | Reserve images for title/section slides; use shapes and color for visual interest on content slides |
| Bright, saturated accent colors | Feels energetic rather than authoritative | Use muted accents that contrast without shouting |
| Asymmetric or unconventional layouts | Business audiences expect predictable structure; asymmetry feels creative, not corporate | Use grid-based, symmetric layouts |
| Oversized titles (44pt+) | Reads as marketing copy, not a report | Keep titles at 36-40pt |
| Heavy use of icons or illustrations | Feels educational or startup-y, not executive | Use icons sparingly if at all; let data and text carry the slide |
| Slides with more imagery than data | Business audiences came for information, not visuals | Every content slide should lead with its data or key message |

## Non-obvious commands

Some ppt-cli workflows that are particularly relevant for business decks and may not be immediately apparent from `--help`:

- **Dark slide backgrounds** are created by adding an empty textbox sized to the full slide, then filling it with `set-fill`. Text shapes added afterward will layer on top. Remember to set light font colors on dark backgrounds.
- **Accent bars** are empty textboxes with a very small width (e.g., 0.08") and a fill color. Use these as side borders on content cards or as thin horizontal dividers.
- **Stat callout layouts** use large font sizes (60-66pt, bold) for the number and small font sizes (14pt, muted color) for the label directly below. Each number-label pair is two separate shapes for independent positioning.
- **Logo placement** on every slide is done by adding an image to each slide at the same coordinates. Place it last on each slide so it layers on top of other elements.
